Abstract 

17 

Crop sowing date information is important for driving crop models and estimating crop yield. 

18 

However, data on sowing dates are often scarce for both developed and developing countries, 

19 

making remote sensing approaches to map sowing date an attractive approach. Yet the relative 

20 

merits of different satellite sensors and metrics to estimate sowing date have not been well 

21 

studied. We assess and compare the accuracies and uncertainties of satellite-derived sowing date 

22 

estimates using different metrics (inflection point and threshold approaches) and satellite sensors 

23 

that cover the range in the electromagnetic spectrum (optical, fluorescence, and radar). We 

24 

validate these estimates using a county-level dataset derived from area-weighted field-level 

25 

reported sowing dates for maize and soybean in the US states of Iowa, Illinois, and Indiana, and 

26 

conduct our analysis both at the county level and at the aggregated tri-state region. We 

27 

specifically use the Enhanced Vegetation Index (EVI) from Moderate Resolution Imaging 

28 

Spectroradiometer (MODIS), solar-induced fluorescence (SIF) from Global Ozone Monitoring 

29 

Experiment-2 (GOME-2) and Ku-band backscattering from QuikSCAT (dB). We also compare 

30 

the inflection point method and the threshold approach that defines sowing date as a specific 

31 

threshold value (10%, 30% and 50%) between the minimum and maximum value in a logistic-

32 

curve smoothed phenology. We find that satellite-based estimate of sowing dates from all 

33 

methods and sensors shows a wide range of predictive power at the county level, with R2 ranging 

34 

between 0.15 and 0.90, with significant variation across sensors. SIF has excellent prediction for 

35 

more counties than the other sensors, but EVI performs more consistently with moderate to good 

36 

prediction for the greatest bulk of counties among all sensors, while dB’s predicted sowing dates 

37 

are negatively correlated with observed dates for most counties. For each sensor,  aggregation to 

38 

the regional level produces time series of predicted sowing dates that more successfully capture 

39 

linear time trends and inter-annual variability. Adjusting for spring temperatures and crop areal 

40 

coverage significantly improves the accuracies of the estimated sowing dates at both the county 

 

2 

41 

and regional scales. Among the combinations of the three satellite products and four metrics, we 

42 

find using SIF and/or EVI and the 30% threshold metric have the highest accuracies in terms of 

43 

reproducing inter-annual variability and minimizing RMSE at regional scales. This study 

44 

provides a systematic assessment of using different satellite sensors and metrics to estimate 

45 

county- to regional-scale crop sowing dates, which has important implications for mapping 

46 

sowing date in data-limited regions across the globe. 

47 

Key Words: sowing date, EVI, solar-induced fluorescence, backscatter, U.S. Midwest

1. Introduction 

50 

Sowing date has been found to be an important factor influencing the final yield of many 

51 

crops, including rice, wheat, and maize, in multiple regions across the globe (Barraclough & 

52 

Leigh, 1984; Cirilo & Andrade, 1996; Lansigan et al., 2000; Lobell & Ortiz-Monasterio, 2007). 

53 

One reason for this strong relationship is that sowing date mediates the weather experienced by a 

54 

crop throughout the growing season, which can have large impacts on crop’s physiology and 

55 

growth (Ortiz-Monasterio R. et al., 1994; Otegui et al., 1995). For example, studies have shown 

56 

that in northern India, wheat yield decreases by approximately 1% for every day of late planting 

57 

(Ortiz-Monasterio R. et al., 1994), primarily because the crop is more likely to experience 

58 

terminal heat stress during the critical grain filling period (Reynolds et al., 1994; Farooq et al., 

59 

2011). Similar cases have been found in the corn-growing regions of the U.S. Midwest where 

60 

average corn yields tend to decrease with later sowing, primarily due to decreased moisture 

61 

availability and increased heat stress during the vegetation and reproductive stages (Irwin et al., 

62 

2015; Nafziger, 2015). Estimating sowing date is also important as it is a key parameter used in 

63 

process-based or empirical crop models to quantify crop yields (Moulin et al., 1998; Lobell et al., 

64 

2009). Given the importance of sowing date for understanding final yield outcomes, it would be 

 

3 

65 

beneficial to identify current sowing dates, sowing date trends through time, and what impacts of 

66 

shifting sowing dates are on yields for a variety of crops at regional to global scales.  

67 

Despite the agronomic importance of sowing date, few census datasets collect this 

68 

information or release these data to the public. Even when these data are released, they are often 

69 

shared at aggregated regional scales (e.g. district- or state-level statistics produced by the US 

70 

Department of Agriculture (USDA) Crop Progress Report). Many studies have therefore relied on 

71 

satellite imagery to map sowing date at fine spatial resolutions across large spatial and temporal 

72 

scales (Sakamoto et al., 2005; Lobell & Ortiz-Monasterio, 2007; Lobell et al., 2012). Different 

73 

satellite datasets, particularly high-temporal resolution products like MODIS, have been used to 

74 

map crop phenology and estimate sowing date of a given crop. Satellite sensors detect crop 

75 

phenology by measuring changes in crop color or biomass through either reflectance from optical 

76 

remote sensing (Moulin et al., 1998), backscattering from active microwave (Vereecken et al., 

77 

2012), or passive microwave emission (Jones et al., 2011). Since crop emergence, which can be 

78 

detected by satellites, usually occurs within a short and relatively constant period after the actual 

79 

sowing date (Lobell et al., 2012), some algorithms have been proposed to extract sowing date 

80 

from remotely sensed phenologies. For example, many studies have shown that the inflection 

81 

point in a MODIS Vegetation Index time series (Zhang et al., 2003) can effectively approximate 

82 

sowing date – these results have been shown to hold true for multiple crops, like rice and soybean, 

83 

in various regions across the globe (Sakamoto et al., 2006; Wardlow et al., 2006; Galford et al., 

84 

2008; Jain et al., 2017). Other studies have shown that sowing date can be better approximated by 

85 

identifying the period when a critical amount of biomass has been achieved during the crop’s 

86 

growth cycle (Boschetti et al., 2009; Lobell et al., 2012). While there are many studies that apply 

87 

a range of metrics to estimate green-up or leaf-flushing for natural ecosystems (White et al., 2009; 

88 

Guan et al., 2014a), few studies have compared multiple methods to map sowing date for 

89 

croplands. Since multiple metrics have been shown to successfully map sowing dates in 

 

4 

90 

individual studies, it is important to compare these various metrics and develop a more 

91 

mechanistic understanding of the possible differences produced by these various metrics.  

92 

Furthermore, while satellite data have been used for decades to map sowing dates, previous 

93 

studies have primarily relied on Vegetation Indices (VIs) calculated from visible and near-

94 

infrared (NIR) bands to detect vegetation green up through the crop’s growth cycle (Sakamoto et 

95 

al., 2005; Wardlow et al., 2006; Galford et al., 2008). Though this approach has been widely used 

96 

and has effectively mapped sowing date in previous studies, satellite data at other spectral ranges 

97 

have been much less explored for this purpose. Specifically, radar data (e.g. backscattering 

98 

signals from active microwave) and the newly emergent solar-induced fluorescence (SIF) data are 

99 

of particular interest, as they both contain different and complementary information to the VIs 

100 

(Guan et al., 2017). For example, VIs are related to the vegetation greenness and photosynthesis 

101 

potential (Sellers et al., 1992). Radar backscattering, on the other hand, provides more 

102 

information related to the water contents in the canopy and/or landscape (Ulaby, 1987; Vereecken 

103 

et al., 2012). Finally, SIF directly detects photosynthetic activity (e.g. electron transport rate) 

104 

(Guanter et al., 2014; van der Tol et al., 2014; Guan et al., 2016). As more new satellite sensors 

105 

have been recently launched or will be in the near future (e.g. the onboard OCO-2 for SIF, 

106 

Sentinel-1 for C-band backscattering), understanding the potential uses of these existing and/or 

107 

new datasets will be important for furthering crop-monitoring applications.  

108 

Our study uses methods from previous studies (e.g. inflection, thresholds of maximum value 

109 

in a phenology) as well as different satellite sensors (e.g. optical, radar, and fluorescence) to 

110 

identify the most effective way to map sowing date for maize and soybean in the Corn Belt of the 

111 

United States. We specifically focus our study in the states of Iowa, Indiana, and Illinois, as we 

112 

have a rich validation dataset of maize and soybean sowing dates at the county level across this 

113 

region. We are primarily interested in assessing which method and product combination can best 

114 

map (1) absolute sowing dates, (2) sowing date spatial patterns, and (3) temporal variability in 

 

5 

115 

sowing dates from 2000 to 2012. To our best knowledge, this is one of the first studies that has 

116 

compared multiple satellite products, such as radar and fluorescence, with more traditional optical 

117 

remote sensing products, such as MODIS EVI, to map sowing date. While our results are likely 

118 

specific to corn and soybean in the Midwestern United States, we also discuss which factors, such 

119 

as weather variations, can lead to bias in our sowing date estimates. Thus, by identifying the 

120 

possible mechanisms for biases across different sensor-by-metric combinations, we aim to 

121 

develop a more general understanding of which method(s) or product(s) can be used to accurately 

122 

map sowing dates for different crops and in different regions at the regional to global scales.

2. Materials and Methods 

125 

2.1 Validation data 

126 

We used a partially sampled dataset from the Risk Management Agency (RMA) of USDA which 

127 

contains 100 randomly sampled fields for both maize and soybean for each county in the states of 

128 

Iowa, Indiana, and Illinois for every year from 1995 to 2012 as described in (Lobell et al., 2014). 

129 

This dataset provides a unique opportunity to assess satellite-derived sowing dates at the county 

130 

level, since most publicly reported sowing date statistics are at the state level. We have compared 

131 

these county-level data (aggregated to the state level) with state-level phenology information 

132 

from “Crop Progress Reports” from the USDA’s National Agricultural Statistics Service (NASS). 

133 

Though our study focuses on county-level results, the comparison to an independent state-level 

134 

dataset provides a useful check, and we find that RMA sowing dates when aggregated to the state 

135 

level correspond to weeks of the year with between 40 and 60 percent area being reported as 

136 

planted according to NASS.

(2) GOME-2 SIF

Solar-induced fluorescence is the energy emitted from the canopy chlorophyll during 

181 

photosynthesis (Berry et al., 2013). Recent advances in spectrometry and retrieval algorithms 

182 

allow the estimation of this variable using satellites (Guanter et al., 2010; Frankenberg et al., 

183 

2011; Joiner et al., 2012, 2013). SIF has been found to be correlated with the electron transport 

184 

rate of photosynthesis (Zhang et al., 2014). Theoretically, SIF can be expressed as:  

185 

SIF=PAR×fPAR×LUESIF 

 

 

 

 

(3),  

186 

where PAR refers to the photosynthetic active radiation and LUESIF is the light use efficiency 

187 

(LUE) of fluorescence. Gross primary production (GPP), or photosynthesis, can be expressed as  

188 

GPP= PAR×fPAR×LUEGPP 

 

 

 

 

(4),  

189 

where LUESIF is the light use efficiency (LUE) of photosynthesis. Though the relationship 

190 

between LUESIF and LUEGPP is not always linear (Porcar-Castell et al., 2014; van der Tol et al., 

191 

2014) and requires further study, when accounting for the contribution of PAR×fPAR, SIF and 

192 

GPP have been found to be linearly related at the canopy (Zarco-Tejada et al., 2012; Rossini et al., 

193 

2015), landscape (Damm et al., 2015; Rascher et al., 2015; Yang et al., 2015) and satellite scales 

194 

(Frankenberg et al., 2011; Guanter et al., 2014). Thus in theory, SIF contains more information 

195 

about vegetation photosynthetic activities than VIs, and a study that examined major eddy-

196 

covariance towers in North America has found that SIF can better track GPP seasonality than 

197 

fPAR and ecosystem models (Joiner et al., 2014).  

198 

In this study, we used SIF retrieved near the λ=740nm spectral window from the Global Ozone 

199 

Monitoring Experiment-2 (GOME-2) instrument onboard Eumetsat’s MetOp-A satellite, which 

200 

has been functioning since 2007 until the present. The instrument’s algorithm (Joiner et al., 2013) 

201 

disentangles three spectral components near the peak of the far-red chlorophyll fluorescence 

202 

emissions feature: atmospheric absorption (due to water vapor), surface reflectance, and 

203 

fluorescence radiance. The derived GOME-2 SIF (version 26) that collected data from 2007-2012 

 

9 

204 

was used in this study. The GOME-2 SIF has the generic footprint of ~40×80 km2, and it has 

205 

been gridded to 0.5° and monthly resolution, with estimated errors of 0.1-0.4 mW/m2/nm/sr 

206 

(Joiner et al., 2013).

(3) QuikSCAT backscatter 

209 

Radar backscattering signals are sensitive to the dialectic properties and surface roughness of the 

210 

landscape (Ulaby et al., 1990). This sensitivity varies with wavelengths. For relatively short 

211 

wavelengths (e.g. X-band: 8-12 GHz, 2.5-3.8 cm; or Ku-band: 12-18 GHz, 1.7-2.5 cm) 

212 

(Vereecken et al., 2012), backscattering signals are especially sensitive to canopy water content 

213 

and biomass (Frolking et al., 2006; Saatchi et al., 2013). With increased wavelengths (e.g. C-

214 

band: 4-8 GHz, 3.8-7.5 cm; L-band: 1-2 GHz, 15-30cm), backscattering signals can partially or 

215 

mostly penetrate the canopy and are thus more sensitive to shallow-layer soil moisture (Jackson, 

216 

1993). Ku-band backscattering data, due to its strong sensitivity to canopy water content and 

217 

biomass, has been used to study vegetation dynamics and phenology in natural ecosystems, 

218 

including tropical forests (Frolking et al., 2012; Guan et al., 2013; Saatchi et al., 2013), tropical 

219 

savannas (Guan et al., 2014b), temperate ecosystems (Frolking et al., 2005, 2006), and boreal 

220 

forests (Kimball et al., 2004). However, the use of radar data in agriculture systems is heavily 

221 

focused on classifying crop types (Mcnairn & Brisco, 2004; Vereecken et al., 2012), and few 

222 

studies have been done to use radar data to measure crop phenology (Lopez-sanchez et al., 2012).  

223 

Here we used Ku-band backscattering data from the QuikSCAT scatterometer. 

224 

QuikSCAT is a Ku-band (13.4 GHz, or 2.1 cm wavelength) active radar with two rotating pencil 

225 

beam antennas operating in H and V polarizations at incidence angles of 55.8° and 46.8°. 

226 

Originally designed for estimating ocean wind fields, QuikSCAT provides the Ku-band 

227 

backscatter coefficient (called “dB” hereafter) over land. The QuikSCAT dB product combines 

 

10 

228 

ascending and descending overpass together (0600 and 1800 h) for a daily composite (Long & 

229 

Early, 2001). VV polarization backscattering was used here due to its high sensitivity to canopy 

230 

water contents (Frolking et al., 2006). QuikSCAT was launched in 1999 and operated until 

231 

November 2009 due to the failure of its antenna spinning motor. Continuation of QuikSCAT was 

232 

partially achieved by the launch of RapidSCAT by NASA that functioned from September 2014 

233 

to August 2016 (Rodriguez, 2013). The current study uses the full record of QuikSCAT dB data 

234 

from 1999 to 2009.

3. Results  

281 

We analyzed each satellite product (dB, EVI, and SIF) and each metric (Tinflection, T10, T20, T30, 

282 

and T50) with respect to both the (i) absolute differences, and (ii) R2 from ordinary least squares 

283 

linear regression (or adjusted R2 to account for uneven sample size) between the observed and 

284 

predicted sowing dates. The former measure represents the bias in a given sensor-metric 

285 

combination, while the latter measure represents how well that metric captures the observed inter-

286 

annual variability in sowing date. Both measures are important for an inter-comparison of 

287 

different products and metrics, since success considering one measure does not necessarily 

288 

guarantee success considering the other, and the utility of each measure depends on the intended 

289 

application. 

290 

 

291 

3.1 Inter-comparison of different sensor-metric combinations at the regional scale 

292 

Figure 1 shows the bias and variability between observed and predicted sowing dates for all the 

293 

sensor-metric combinations. Overall, SIF achieves the best performance (cor = 0.93) for Tinflection, 

294 

T30 and T50, which outperforms EVI by a significant margin (difference of cor = 0.15). This 

295 

result confirms previous findings that SIF can better track plant growth because SIF captures 

 

13 

296 

photosynthetic activity rather than color represented by EVI or other VIs (Joiner et al., 2014). 

297 

Considering dB, only the T50 metric achieves a reasonable performance (cor = 0.35) while the 

298 

other metrics using dB show negative correlations with observed data. This indicates that dB’s 

299 

time series contain large uncertainties especially in the initial green-up stage, and it only becomes 

300 

more stable and reliable when dB passes 50% of the green-up stage (i.e. T50). For the RMSE, we 

301 

find that Tinflection and T30 always achieve the lowest RMSE, consistent across all three satellite 

302 

products. This largely indicates that these two metrics may be more suitable for estimating the 

303 

absolute values of sowing dates.

3.2 Impacts of maize-soybean area ratio 

360 

We examined whether the ratio of maize to soybean at the county-level influenced estimated 

361 

sowing date accuracy. It was necessary for us to examine the relative influence of soybean versus 

362 

maize on our estimation accuracies because our validation data were available at the county-level, 

363 

where maize and soybean are both planted, and all satellite products were used at coarse spatial 

364 

resolutions (>1km) resulting in mixed pixels. Furthermore, there are likely phenological 

365 

differences in maize and soybean, as maize is sown, on average, two weeks earlier than soybean 

366 

in this region, and any given satellite signal reflects the green-up from both crops. 

367 

Our results show that satellite-estimated sowing dates are not sensitive to variability in the ratio 

368 

of maize to soybean area (Figure 4). While there has been a small trend toward increased maize 

369 

since 2000, we see that the correlation between county-level predicted and observed sowing dates 

370 

bears little relation to crop ratio, and that aggregate maize and soybean sowing dates are highly 

371 

correlated (R2 = 0.86) (Figure 3). The relative contributions of maize and soybean to the 

372 

aggregate predicted sowing date do not vary significantly across years, which removes the 

373 

possible confounding effect of increased area under maize through time.

476

477

478

479

3.4 Integrating temperature and satellite estimation to infer linear trends and inter-annual 

499 

variability at regional scales 

500 

While strong correlation exists between observed and predicted sowing dates for some individual 

501 

counties, the large spread of correlations at the county level suggests that additional, location-

502 

specific information must be integrated with satellite measures to obtain more consistent sowing 

503 

date prediction at this spatial scale. However, the much higher correlations we find at the tri-state 

504 

level suggest that satellite-based methods show significant promise for estimating the magnitude 

505 

of linear trends and inter-annual variability in regional sowing dates. This is especially important 

506 

given that these methods are most likely to be desirable in areas where local ground-based sowing 

507 

date reporting, and sometimes even local weather data, are difficult to obtain. For EVI’s T30 

508 

metric, we find an R2 of 0.57 when predicting regional sowing date based on satellite data alone. 

509 

This is improved to 0.71 when the maize to soybean ratio is added to the model, and to 0.78 when 

510 

adding the average temperature during planting months (April and May, in this study).

512 

4. Discussion 

513 

Sowing date information is critical for crop modeling and yield estimation, but spatially-explicit 

514 

sowing date information is lacking even in developed countries, such as the US Midwest, which 

515 

produces about 45% of global maize and 30% of global soybean production. In this study, we 

516 

provide a systematic assessment using multiple satellite products and different retrieval metrics to 

517 

estimate crop sowing date at the county level for the three major states (Illinois, Indiana, and 

518 

Iowa) in the US Midwest that have a dominant landscape of maize and soybean. We use sowing 

 

28 

519 

dates aggregated to the county level from the randomly sampled RMA field-level data to validate 

520 

our remote sensing estimates of sowing date.  

521 

Our results suggest that the three satellite products (EVI, SIF and Ku-dB) have variable abilities 

522 

to capture inter-annual variability of sowing dates at county or regionally aggregated levels. Both 

523 

EVI- and SIF-based estimations of sowing dates using T30 and Tinflection  achieve very high 

524 

accuracies (adjusted R2 >0.75, RMSE < 10 days), while dB-based estimates have much lower 

525 

accuracies. In general SIF-based estimates outperform EVI-based estimates, particularly at the 

526 

regional scale (Figure 1 & 2). This is likely because SIF captures actual photosynthetic activity 

527 

(Joiner et al., 2014) as opposed to only color as seen by VIs like EVI (Berry et al., 2013; Porcar-

528 

Castell et al., 2014). However, we also find that SIF contains more noise at the county level 

529 

compared to EVI (Figures 5 and Figure 6), which leads to lower accuracies through time than 

530 

EVI at finer spatial scales. The higher level of noise in SIF is primarily due to the sensor’s low 

531 

sampling frequency and coarse spatial resolution, which will be improved on with new satellite 

532 

missions, such as TROPOMI (Guanter et al., 2015), FLEX (Rascher et al., 2008), and OCO-2/-3 

533 

(Frankenberg et al., 2014).  

534 

Regarding the different metrics considered in our study, i.e. the inflection method and the 

535 

threshold approach, we find that the threshold approach at 30% had the highest accuracies across 

536 

data products and measures of accuracy (adjusted R2, RMSE, and correlation of anomalies 

537 

through time). We also find that for sowing date estimates derived using dB, the T50 approach 

538 

was the most accurate. This is likely because the dB time series contain large uncertainties in the 

539 

initial green-up stage and only becomes more stable when dB passes 50% of the green-up stage. 

540 

We examined the influence of multiple potential confounding factors on the accuracies of our 

541 

sowing date estimates. When considering the relative area in a county planted under maize and 

542 

soybean, we find that there is high inter-annual correlation between maize and soybean (Figure 

543 

4c), and the maize-soybean ratio is not associated with bias in our sowing date estimates (Figure 

 

29 

544 

4b). However, we do find that crop fraction (i.e. natural vegetation fraction) and sowing period 

545 

temperature have significant impacts on estimated sowing dates (Figure 7). Specifically, an 

546 

increased fraction of natural vegetation in a pixel leads to an earlier estimated sowing date from 

547 

the satellite data compared to observed data, likely due to the early green-up of natural vegetation 

548 

compared to crop cover in the spring. Higher sowing-period temperature leads to a later estimated 

549 

sowing date compared to observed data, and this is because warmer temperatures speed up 

550 

emergence. Future work may be able to reduce the confounding effects of mixed pixels by using 

551 

finer spatial resolution data, such as Landsat (30 meter) and Sentinel-2 (10-20 meter), likely 

552 

allowing for field-level accuracy. We find that incorporating crop area fraction and sowing-period 

553 

temperature in our model that estimates sowing date using satellite data leads to the highest 

554 

accuracies, with better R2 and lower RMSE than any of the models that use only satellite data to 

555 

estimate sowing date.  

556 

 

557 

5. Conclusion 

558 

Overall our study finds that satellite measures can capture sowing date variation in both space and 

559 

time, with varying degrees of accuracy for different satellites, metrics, and spatial scales (e.g. 

560 

county vs. region). We generally find poor performance for the dB product, but promising results 

561 

from both EVI and SIF. At the county level, SIF has excellent prediction (correlation > .8) for 

562 

more counties than EVI (57 vs. 17, respectively), but EVI has moderate to good prediction (.3 < 

563 

correlation < .8) for more counties than SIF (190 vs. 144), and also fewer counties with negative 

564 

correlation (16 vs. 32). These products successfully capture aggregate time trends and interannual 

565 

variability at the regional level, though their success is mixed at the county level, where county-

566 

specific factors such as natural vegetation to crop ratio can affect both the bias and ability to 

567 

capture trends and internnual variability. While SIF exhibits less sensitivity to natural vegetation 

568 

than EVI, its time series is too short to adequately assess whether it truly outperforms EVI. At 

 

30 

569 

both county and regional scales, removing biases to predict absolute sow dates is possible by 

570 

careful choice of metric (in this study, T30 did the best), but highly sensitive to spring 

571 

temperature and crop to natural vegetation ratio, and thus not likely to extrapolate to new data. 

572 

We therefore see identifying regional aggregate trends and variability as the most likely use case 

573 

for this approach.
